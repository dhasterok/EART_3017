\begin{activity}{Visualizing Magnetic Fields}

\section*{Purpose}

This activity develops physical intuition for magnetic fields as vector fields generated by dipoles. You will explore how field geometry depends on source orientation, on the interaction between multiple sources, and on how far you are from the source.

\section*{Learning Outcomes}

By the end of this activity, you should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Describe and sketch the field pattern of a single magnetic dipole in vertical and horizontal orientations.
  \item Predict and compare the field pattern produced by two interacting dipole sources.
  \item Identify how the field pattern near the end of a stacked line of magnets differs from the pattern along its side, and relate this to a finite geological structure.
  \item Explain why source orientation, not source strength alone, determines the shape of a magnetic anomaly.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Background}

Iron filings align with the local field direction, so the pattern they form shows you field-line direction and curvature --- where lines converge, diverge, and close. What filings do \emph{not} give you is a reliable measure of field strength: filing density is too easily affected by how much you sprinkle and how the paper is tapped to treat as data. Throughout this activity, base your answers on field \emph{shape and symmetry}, not on how densely the filings appear to cluster.

\section*{Key Ideas}

\textbf{Magnetic fields are dipolar, not monopolar.} Every magnet has both a north and south pole; the field lines form closed loops. This gives magnetic anomalies a more complex shape than gravity anomalies, which arise from monopole-like mass distributions.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[]{figures/monodipole.pdf}
  \caption{The (b) monopolar field of a point mass and the (a) dipolar field of a magnetic point source.}
  \label{fig:polar}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Orientation controls anomaly shape.} Rotating the same source changes the pattern of positive and negative lobes without changing the source volume or susceptibility. This is the fundamental reason why magnetic interpretation requires knowledge of the inducing field direction and source magnetisation direction.

\textbf{A line of aligned dipoles can look like a single pole --- but only up close.} Stacking magnets pole-to-pole cancels every internal north-south junction, leaving only the two outer poles, now separated by the full length of the stack. Close to one end, that far pole is negligible and the field looks like a single isolated pole. Far from the stack, both poles matter again and the field must revert to a two-lobed, dipolar pattern --- because total magnetic charge is always exactly zero, no matter how a source is shaped or arranged. This is the same limit you tested with the cube magnet: no geometry escapes it, only postpones it to shorter range.
