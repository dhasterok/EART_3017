\section*{Purpose}

This workshop builds physical intuition for magnetic fields and their geological sources. Students first build up dipole field geometry from direct observation, then apply that intuition to predict anomaly shapes for idealised structures, before turning to what actually carries magnetisation in real rocks and how thermal structure limits it at depth.

\section*{Learning Outcomes}

By the end of this workshop, students should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Describe and predict magnetic field patterns generated by dipoles and combinations of sources
  \item Predict magnetic anomaly shapes from simple geological geometries, and contrast them with gravity anomalies for the same structures
  \item Relate magnetic susceptibility to rock type and geological process, and distinguish primary from secondary controls on it
  \item Explain the concept of Curie depth and its relationship to thermal structure
  \item Link source depth to anomaly wavelength and spectral content
\end{itemize}

\section*{Session Flow (\timelinelength\ min)}

\timeline[slot]{Warm-Up: Cutting a Magnet in Half}{10}

Thought experiment: if you cut a bar magnet exactly in half, does one piece become a pure ``north'' and the other a pure ``south''?

\begin{itemize}
    \item What do you predict happens to each half?
    \item If you kept cutting smaller and smaller pieces, would you ever reach a piece with only one pole?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Target idea:} Every magnetic source, no matter how small, has both a north and south pole --- an isolated magnetic pole has never been observed. Keep this in mind today: later in the session you'll meet a case where a source's field \emph{looks} like it has only one pole. See if you can work out how that's possible if isolated poles don't exist.

\timeline[item]{Visualizing Magnetic Fields}{35}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Iron filings with a single disc magnet, vertical and horizontal orientation
  \item Predict-observe-compare-reflect: two dipoles side by side
  \item A line of stacked magnets --- field pattern near one end vs.\ along the side
  \item Optional extension: cube magnets, and whether shape alone can remove dipolar behaviour at range
\end{itemize}

\timeline[slot]{Mini-Lecture: Naming What You Found}{15}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Formalising vocabulary: dipole vs.\ monopole, why isolated magnetic poles cannot exist
  \item Dipole convergence figures: two opposite point poles at decreasing separation, showing how a monopole-like appearance at close range gives way to a dipole pattern at distance
  \item How magnetics differs from gravity (vector nature, polarity)
  \item Preview: applying this field geometry to predict anomalies from real geological structures
\end{itemize}

\timeline[item]{Magnetic Anomalies vs.\ Gravity}{40}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Reasoning-based sketching of anomalies for idealised geometries (sphere, cylinder, slab)
  \item Direct comparison with gravity anomalies for the same bodies
  \item Vertical vs.\ horizontal inducing field
\end{itemize}

\timeline[slot]{Break}{10}

\timeline[slot]{Warm-Up: Which Property Matters Most?}{10}

Suppose you could know only one property of every rock in the crust. Which would best help predict magnetic anomalies: FeO concentration, magnetite abundance, magnetic susceptibility, density, remanent magnetization, or oxidation state?

\textbf{Target idea:} There isn't a perfect answer --- each property captures part of the picture but misses something else. Today's activity will show why susceptibility depends on a trace mineral phase rather than on any single bulk property.

\timeline[item]{Magnetic Susceptibility of Rocks}{45}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Station 1: reaction pairs (peridotite/serpentinite, basalt/altered basalt, eclogite) --- magnetite created or destroyed by reaction
  \item Station 2: igneous suite --- magnetite set by primary crystallisation, no reaction involved
  \item Station 3: ore minerals --- decoupling metal content from magnetism
\end{itemize}

\timeline[item]{Curie Depth and Magnetic Crust}{50}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Curie temperature and magnetic crustal thickness
  \item Depth--wavelength relationships
  \item Antarctica case study
  \item Back-of-the-envelope heat-flow estimates
\end{itemize}

\timeline[slot]{Wrap-up}{15}
\begin{itemize}
  \item What magnetics can and cannot tell us
  \item Connection to upcoming Fourier/spectral methods
\end{itemize}

\timeline[slot]{Preview: Fourier Transforms and Potential Fields}{10}

Both gravity and magnetic anomalies contain information about source depth encoded in their spatial wavelengths: deep sources produce broad, long-wavelength anomalies while shallow sources produce narrow, short-wavelength anomalies. Next week we formalise this depth--wavelength relationship using Fourier transforms. We will see how gravity and magnetic data can be filtered, upward-continued, and reduced-to-the-pole in the wavenumber domain, and how sampling decisions control what geological features can be resolved.
