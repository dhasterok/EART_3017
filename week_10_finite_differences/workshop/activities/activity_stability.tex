\begin{activity}{Stability, Time Stepping, and Information Transfer}

\section*{Purpose}
To develop physical intuition for numerical stability in explicit finite
difference schemes.

In a time-stepping model, temperature is updated step-by-step. Each step uses information from neighbouring nodes.

Think of heat as spreading gradually through the system.

\section*{Part A: Prediction}

\textbf{Consider two cases:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item very small time steps
    \item very large time steps
\end{itemize}

What do you expect to happen in each case?

\section*{Part B: Physical reasoning}

\textbf{Task:}
Explain why heat cannot move arbitrarily far in a single time step.

What determines how quickly heat diffuses through a material?

\section*{Part C: Stability condition}

For a one-dimensional explicit scheme:
\begin{equation}
\Delta t < \frac{\Delta z^2}{2\kappa}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Tasks:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Why does the allowable time step depend on $\Delta z$?
    \item Why does it depend on thermal diffusivity $\kappa$?
\end{itemize}

\section*{Part D: Interpretation}

\textbf{Task:}
Interpret the stability condition as a limit on how fast thermal information can move between nodes.

Why can heat not ``jump'' over nodes in a single time step?

\section*{Part E: Geological interpretation}

\textbf{Explain:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Why are sedimentary basins (low $\kappa$) easier to model?
    \item Why do high-diffusivity materials require smaller time steps?
\end{itemize}

\bigskip

\textbf{Key Insight} \\
Numerical instability occurs when the model allows heat to propagate faster
than physically possible.

\end{activity}
