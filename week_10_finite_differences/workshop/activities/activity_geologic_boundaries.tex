\begin{activity}{Boundary Conditions and Geological Meaning}

\section*{Purpose}
To understand how boundary conditions encode geological assumptions in numerical
models.

A numerical model does not ``know'' geology—it only knows equations and boundary
conditions.

\emph{Boundary conditions determine what kind of Earth your model represents.}

\section*{Part A: Grounding in real systems}

Consider the following settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Ocean floor beneath deep water
    \item Continental lithosphere
    \item A hot magma body cooling in the crust
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Task:}
For each case, describe what controls the temperature at the boundaries.

\section*{Part B: Types of boundary conditions}

Common thermal boundary conditions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Fixed temperature (Dirichlet)
    \item Fixed heat flux (Neumann)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Task:}
Match each type of boundary condition to a geological situation.

\section*{Part C: Comparing assumptions}

\textbf{Explain} how the model changes if you prescribe:
\begin{itemize}
    \item surface temperature
    \item surface heat flow
\end{itemize}

What is fixed in each case? What is allowed to vary?

\section*{Part D: Geological consequences}

\textbf{Compare} appropriate boundary conditions for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item oceanic lithosphere
    \item continental lithosphere
\end{itemize}

\section*{Part E: Critical thinking}

\textbf{Task:}
Explain why inappropriate boundary conditions can produce physically misleading
results—even if the numerical solution is mathematically stable.

\textbf{Reflection} \\
If two models use the same equation but different boundary conditions, can they
produce completely different results? Why?

\bigskip

\textbf{Key Insight} \\
Boundary conditions are where geological assumptions enter a numerical model.

\end{activity}
